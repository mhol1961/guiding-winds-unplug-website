Most pack-lists for a catamaran charter are written by someone who has never lived on a boat for a week. They tell you to bring a robe and a pair of deck shoes, then forget to mention that you cannot, in any meaningful sense, blow-dry your hair at anchor, and that the deck shoes you bought for the trip will be in the bottom of your duffel by Tuesday. We've hosted enough voyages now to know what people quietly wish they'd packed by midweek — and what they wish they'd left at home. The list below is what we tell friends. It is also, after fourteen voyages a year, the closest thing we have to a manifesto on what to pack catamaran charter style without overpacking.

## The seven you wish you had

These are the items guests message us about on the third day. The phrasing varies; the regret doesn't.

**Reef-safe sunscreen.** The British Virgin Islands have been reef-strict by law since 2020 — oxybenzone and octinoxate are banned, and customs will pull a bottle from your bag at Beef Island if they catch it. Stream2Sea, Raw Elements, and Thinksport all pass. We keep extras on board, but you'll want your own bottle in your daypack for shore stops. If your tube reads "broad spectrum" without listing zinc or titanium, leave it home.

**A quick-dry sarong.** One piece of fabric does the work of six items. Cover-up over a swimsuit on the dinghy ride to shore. Towel for the salty seat back to the boat. Picnic blanket on a Sandy Spit beach. Shoulder wrap when the wind picks up at dinner. Linen or microfiber, not terry. The kind you can wring out and re-roll in your dry bag without thinking about it.

**A hat with a chin strap.** Your favorite hat without a strap is going overboard by Tuesday. We've watched it happen with straw hats, ball caps, panamas, expensive felt fedoras. Two we recommend: the Outdoor Research Sombriolet (synthetic, packable, looks better than it sounds) or the Tilley T3 in the wider-brim cut. Both have removable chin cords. Both survive a saltwater rinse.

**Water shoes.** The Anegada reef flats are not barefoot. Neither is the rocky landing at Sandy Cay, neither are most of the Mediterranean coves we'll work into later seasons. Closed-toe water shoes — Astral Loyak, Keen Newport H2, or the simple Vivobarefoot Ultra — protect against sea urchins, sharp coral, and the occasional dock splinter. One pair is enough.

**A small dry bag, 15 to 20 liters.** Big enough for your phone, your wallet, a paperback, and a change of clothes for the shore dinner. Small enough to live in the bottom of your cabin without taking a cubic foot. Sea to Summit, Watershed, and NRS all make versions that survive a full season. The roll-top closure is non-negotiable — zippered dry bags lie.

**A refillable water bottle.** We have filtered water on board and we don't stock disposable plastic. A 24-ounce insulated bottle (Hydro Flask, YETI Rambler, Owala Free Sip) keeps water cold from breakfast until the afternoon swim. Bring one per person. Label it with tape — by Wednesday everyone's identical bottles get confused at the helm.

**Motion-sickness patches, if you're prone.** Scopolamine patches are prescription-only. The dose is 1.5mg, applied behind the ear roughly four hours before departure, and they last about three days. Ask your doctor before you fly — most will write a prescription for a charter without a fuss. The patches work better than Dramamine and don't make you drowsy. We carry Bonine on board as a backup, but you cannot get scopolamine after you land in Tortola without a long detour. Catamarans are dramatically more stable than monohulls and the BVI is one of the most protected sailing grounds on earth, but if cruise ships make you queasy, the patch is your insurance policy.

## The nine you can leave at home

Here's where most BVI packing list articles go wrong — they pad the bag with things you'll never touch. Don't bring these.

1. **Formal clothes.** The nicest place we'll eat all week is a barefoot tavern at White Bay. Linen, soft cotton, and a single sundress or a clean button-down cover every dinner. No jackets. No closed shoes after the first night.
2. **A hair dryer.** The boat has one in the main head. Cabin counter space is genuinely tight, and a second dryer doesn't fit anywhere it won't bounce around at anchor.
3. **Snorkel gear.** We provision masks, fins, and snorkels in every size from small youth to XXL. Prescription mask inserts are the one exception — if you wear contacts and prefer not to swim in them, bring your own mask.
4. **Beach towels.** Provided. Two per guest, swapped midweek. Hotel-quality, oversized, color-coded so we don't mix yours with the next cabin's.
5. **A beach bag.** The boat is the beach bag. The dinghy carries what you need to shore; the dry bag carries what you need on the sand. A second canvas tote becomes one more thing to corral.
6. **Heavy books.** One paperback and a Kindle, maximum. Hardcovers get warped by salt air within 48 hours, and there's no shelf wide enough to hold the stack you packed.
7. **A yoga mat.** Foam pads live on the foredeck for Dodie's morning sessions. They're tacky-side-down, salt-rinsed nightly, and yours for the week.
8. **Valuable jewelry.** Salt corrodes silver and gold filling within days, and the chance of losing an earring in the water is higher than you'd think. The watch you don't mind getting wet, a thin band, a pair of studs. That's the inventory.
9. **More than two pairs of shoes.** One pair of water shoes, one pair of soft slip-ons for shore dinners. A third pair takes up a cabin shelf that should be holding your sunscreen.

## The one item we don't tell you about until you're aboard

Your first night in the cabin, on the pillow, you'll find a hand-written index card. It's from the guest who slept in your bed on the previous voyage — one sentence on what their best moment of the week was. Sometimes it's a sentence about a meal. Sometimes it's a sunrise at Cooper Island. Sometimes it's the swim at the Indians before the day boats arrived. We started doing it after our third charter, when a guest left a thank-you note tucked into the cabin mirror and we realized it had set the tone for the entire week that followed.

Here's how it works. On disembark morning, we leave a blank card and a pen on the chart table. Anyone who wants to writes one — most do, a few don't, both are fine. Dodie sorts them by cabin and tucks them in for the next voyage. You'll never know who wrote yours. You'll never be asked to write one. But if you do, your sentence travels to a stranger you'll never meet, and that's the part nobody expects to love. Bring the version of yourself who'd write a good one.

## A note on luggage

One soft-sided duffel and one daypack. That's the right kit for a catamaran charter packing list, and it's how the crew packs too. Hard suitcases will not fit in the cabin storage — the under-berth and overhead spaces are sized for fabric that compresses, and a wheeled hardshell will spend the week wedged in the salon or strapped to the foredeck where it'll catch spray. Soft duffels fold flat, slide under the bed, and disappear by lunch on day one. A 60- to 80-liter duffel is the sweet spot for a week. Patagonia Black Hole, Yeti Panga, and the L.L. Bean Adventure Duffel are all proven on our boat.

Pack to the size of what you'll bring home, not what you brought. By Friday you'll have a bottle of Pusser's rum from Soper's Hole, a sarong from the Anegada market, a small ceramic from Spanish Town, and a phone full of photos. Leave room. The trip back is always heavier than the trip out — and that's worth a quarter of your duffel held open on the way down.

## What we'll provide

Some of this is in the inclusion list on the voyage page; some isn't. Either way, you don't need to bring duplicates.

- All bed linens and pillows, swapped midweek
- Two beach towels per guest, oversized, color-coded by cabin
- Full snorkel kit per guest (mask, fins, snorkel) in every size
- Two paddleboards, two kayaks, fins for everyone
- Filtered drinking water on tap (no bottled plastic on board)
- Rain shells for the rare afternoon squall
- Full first-aid kit, AED, oxygen, EPIRB
- Two beach umbrellas for long Sandy Spit afternoons
- A small shelf of board games and a deck of cards for the once-a-week rainy hour
- A French press, a pour-over, and a small espresso maker — Dodie loses arguments about which is best, weekly

Pack light. Pack the seven. Skip the nine. The boat is provisioned for the trip, not for the photo of the trip — and the rest, you'll figure out by Wednesday, which is when most guests stop checking what time it is.
