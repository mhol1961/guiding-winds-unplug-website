There are something like sixty named anchorages in the British Virgin Islands, depending on who's counting and whether you include the moorings that only hold three boats. Most charter brochures push the same five - Soggy Dollar, Bitter End, Cooper, The Baths, Norman - and run those five every week for every guest. That's a fine list. It's not the list I'd pick.

I've been running the BVI roughly fourteen weeks a year for the last several seasons. After enough trips you stop reading the brochures and start filtering on what you'd want again if someone told you you only got five. Not the five that photograph best. The five you'd anchor at on a week you were doing for yourself.

Here they are, with the practical notes I'd give a captain friend.

## 1. Norman Island, The Bight

This is the opening note of nearly every BVI week I run. Saturday afternoon, off the dock at Nanny Cay, hard reach east-southeast for about three hours, drop the hook in The Bight before sundown. It's the anchorage that tells guests what the week is going to feel like - protected water, a curve of green hills around you, and a snorkel they'll remember on day one.

The Caves on the south side of the bay are the easy answer. Three sea caves cut into the rock face, fifteen feet deep, full of glassy minnows that part around you in sheets. Every guidebook lists them and they earn the listing. The harder answer, and the better one, is the Indians - three rock pillars a half-mile south of The Bight that erupt straight from the seabed to fifty feet above the waterline. The pillars are old volcanic plugs, the rock around them eroded away over a few million years, leaving these vertical columns ringed with fan coral and reef fish. The swim from the mooring takes maybe thirty minutes round-trip and rewards it. Almost nobody from the day-charter scene bothers; they unload at the Caves and motor off.

Ashore, Pirates Bight is the move for sundown. It's not a destination restaurant, but the rum's cold and the deck faces the right direction.

One practical tip for any Norman Island catamaran approach: anchor on the east side of The Bight, not the middle. You catch the morning wind earlier, which means you swing on your hook before the heat builds, and by 11am when the day-charter raft-up starts forming in the center of the bay you're already out of it.

## 2. The Baths, Virgin Gorda

The Baths is the most-photographed anchorage in the BVI and I'd still pick it. The trick is timing, and the timing is uncompromising: arrive at first light, in the water by 7:30, out by 11.

The play works because the day-tour boats out of Tortola don't show up until nine, sometimes 9:30 if the wind is light. That gives you ninety minutes alone in the rock pools before the crowd lands. The boulders are granite - proper granite, the kind you'd see in New Hampshire - and they sit here because of a deep magma intrusion that cooled underground around seventy million years ago. Erosion stripped the softer rock around them and left these house-sized boulders piled along the shore. Saltwater works its way between them and forms sea pools you can swim through, sometimes ducking under a boulder to surface in the next chamber. No photograph conveys it, which is half the reason guests come back from the swim quiet.

Practical note: there are no overnight moorings inside the national park boundary at The Baths. Don't try to anchor inside; the holding is bad and the rangers will move you. Spend the night before at Spanish Town a mile north, pick up a Devil's Bay mooring at 6am, ferry in by tender. By 11 you're back aboard, hook up, and on the move to Anegada or back toward Cooper depending on the week.

## 3. Anegada

The wildcard. Anegada is the one most charters skip - it requires an overnight passage from Virgin Gorda, the entrance is across a reef pass that has eaten more keels than I want to count, and the island itself is so flat you don't see it until you're three miles off. None of that is a reason to skip it. It's the reason to go.

Anegada is the only coral atoll in the BVI chain. Eleven miles long, fifteen feet at its highest point, ringed by Horseshoe Reef on the south and a pink-sand beach on the north that runs for seven miles. It's geologically a different animal from the rest of the BVI - volcanic everywhere else, sedimentary coral here - and it feels like a different country once you're ashore.

Three reasons we go. First, the lobster. Cow Wreck on the north beach and Potter's by the Sea at Setting Point both grill Caribbean spiny lobster over open coals, split, basted in lemon butter and a pepper rub that varies by cook. It's genuinely the best meal of the week and not a close contest. Second, the bonefish flats on the south side - if you have a guest who fishes, the flats are a sub-trip in themselves, and even non-fishers walk the shallows for an hour and come back with stories. Third, the north-side beach, where on a normal day you'll share seven miles of pink sand with fewer people than the parking lot of an average grocery store.

Captain note for any Anegada catamaran approach: get the current GPS waypoint for the Setting Point entrance before you leave. Setting Point Reef has shifted over the decades and the chartwork lags. The Anegada Reef Hotel monitors VHF 16 and will talk you through the cut if the light isn't right. Don't try the entrance after 3pm in the off-season - the sun is behind you and the reef heads disappear. The reef is unforgiving and the closest Coast Guard cutter is in Puerto Rico.

## 4. Manchioneel Bay, Cooper Island

This is my back-half-of-the-week pick, and the one I'd anchor at last if the week was mine.

Cooper Island BVI is a small, low-population island east of Tortola with one thing on it: the Cooper Island Beach Club, a low-key resort with about ten rooms, a brewpub, a dive shop, and a beach restaurant. The bartender at the beach bar remembers what guests we've brought through. The brewer makes a passable pale ale and a better stout that doesn't travel; you have to drink it here. The dive shop runs the Wreck of the Rhone trip out to Salt Island - one of the best dive sites in the Caribbean, sunk in an 1867 hurricane, sitting in 30 to 80 feet of water - if you have certified divers aboard.

Manchioneel Bay is also the quietest mooring field on our usual circuit. Twenty-five moorings, well spaced, no marina noise, no day-boat traffic after 5pm. The last-night vibe I aim for: long dinner ashore at the beach restaurant, dinghy back at 10pm, kill the rig lights, and the sky goes dark enough you can see the Milky Way arc straight overhead. Nobody talks much on that dinghy ride back. They don't need to.

## 5. Great Harbour, Jost Van Dyke

Not White Bay - White Bay is the famous Jost Van Dyke anchorage, the one with the Soggy Dollar, the photo every charter brochure runs. White Bay is fine. Great Harbour, around the corner on the south side of the island, is better for spending a night.

Great Harbour is calmer. The holding is better. There are fewer boats because most charters drop their hook at White Bay for the Soggy Dollar photo and then move on. And the original Foxy's bar - the one Foxy actually built in 1968, the one that hasn't moved since - sits ashore at Great Harbour. Foxy himself still wanders in on most evenings, tells the same stories he's been telling for forty years, plays a calypso set if the room asks for it. Nobody photographs Foxy's the way they photograph the Soggy Dollar, which is a large part of the appeal.

For any Jost Van Dyke catamaran captain: holding gets thin on the eastern side of Great Harbour where the sand turns to broken coral. Aim for the western half of the bay, look for a clear sand patch, and back down hard on your anchor before you trust it. The bottom looks better than it sets in places. Twenty meters of chain in eight meters of water is the right ratio.

## The honorable mentions

Three I'd add for week-eight guests, the ones who've already done the main five and want something quieter:

**Sandy Spit.** Uninhabited, the size of a city block, ringed by a sandbar you can walk in two minutes. The snorkel on the windward side is thirty minutes of reef and is worth the dinghy ride from anywhere within five miles.

**Diamond Cay.** Mangroves on the north side of Jost Van Dyke. Kayak in at high tide, drift through the channels, watch the bonefish work the roots. Half a day, no crowds, no bar, no agenda.

**Marina Cay.** A small island off Beef Island with a restaurant above the dock that does a fine grilled fish and a sunset view back toward Tortola. The mooring field holds maybe twelve boats and the kitchen closes at nine. Low-key dinner stop, easy night.

Five anchorages won't be enough. They never are. But if you only get five - and most BVI weeks really only have time for five overnights plus a couple of lunch stops - these are the ones I'd give you.
