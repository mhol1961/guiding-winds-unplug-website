Anegada is a flat coral atoll fifteen miles north of the rest of the British Virgin Islands, eleven feet above sea level at its highest point, surrounded by an eighteen-mile reef that has eaten more ships than the local government can count. It is the only island in the chain you can't see from another island. It is also where the dinner of your week happens. Most charter brochures bury the Anegada lobster in the middle of the itinerary, sandwiched between The Baths and Jost Van Dyke like a side note. We put it on the home page because, honestly, it deserves it.

## What the lobster actually is

The Anegada lobster is *Panulirus argus* - the Caribbean spiny lobster. No claws. All tail. Two long, dramatic antennae that move independently of each other and seem to know things the rest of the lobster doesn't. The body is mottled brown and orange with cream spots, and the meat is firmer and sweeter than the cold-water Maine cousin most Americans grew up with. If you've only ever eaten New England lobster, the first bite reads as a different animal entirely. It is.

Spiny lobsters live in the coral crevices and reef rubble around the Anegada flats. In the fall they do something called a "marching procession" - single-file lines of lobsters walking the seabed for reasons marine biologists still argue about. The fishermen on Anegada catch them in baited wooden traps that get checked at dawn, the same way the same families have done it for four generations. The fishery is small-scale and seasonally regulated by the BVI government - closed from April 1 through July 31 to protect the spawning season, with size minimums enforced at the dock. The lobster on your plate at Cow Wreck Beach Bar was almost certainly caught that morning, by a fisherman whose grandfather caught lobsters at the same coordinates.

This matters because the freshness is the dinner. A spiny lobster that hit the water two hours ago tastes nothing like one that's been on ice for three days. The whole reason the Anegada catamaran detour exists in the BVI itinerary is that you can't get this dinner anywhere else.

## Where to eat it

Three places, all good, none of them trying to be anything they aren't.

**Cow Wreck Beach Bar (north shore).** The iconic one. Picnic tables in the sand, a grill made from a halved 55-gallon drum, hand-painted signs, the kind of beach-bar aesthetic Instagram tries to manufacture and Cow Wreck has had since 1985. The lobster is the focus and the rest of the menu admits it - coleslaw, mac salad, johnnycakes, all supporting cast, all fine. Get there from Setting Point by taxi (around $20 round-trip, the driver waits or comes back for you) or by tender if conditions on the north shore allow, which they often don't. Worth the taxi.

**Potter's by the Sea (Setting Point).** A two-minute walk from where the boats anchor. Equally good lobster, marginally better service, slightly worse view - the water at Setting Point is more harbor than horizon. The bar runs longer than dinner, which is the deciding factor for some weeks and some groups. If your night already has momentum and nobody wants to organize a taxi, Potter's is the right call.

**The Anegada Reef Hotel restaurant.** The third option. More formal in a 1970s Caribbean way - tablecloths, a printed menu, the same family who's owned the hotel for three generations running the room. Excellent lobster, good fresh fish daily, a slightly more grown-up evening than the beach-shack versions. If you have a group where someone wants a proper sit-down meal and not a plastic fork in the sand, this is where you go.

You can't go wrong with any of them. We rotate by week and by group mood. The lobster is the same lobster.

## How to order one

A short opinion section, accumulated over seven sailing years and roughly a hundred dinners.

**Order it whole.** They'll offer it halved, which is the lower-commitment option and perfectly acceptable. Whole is what we ate aboard the boat in 2018, the night the entire group decided collectively that halved was a hedge, and we have never gone back. The tail is the headline, but there is meat in the body and the antennae bases that you will only find if you have the whole animal in front of you.

**Ask for it cracked at the table.** They'll bring tools - small wooden mallets, picks, a folded cloth napkin that is not going to survive the evening. You'll use all of it. Cracking the lobster yourself is part of the meal; it slows you down, it gives the table something to do collectively, and it stops the kitchen from over-cooking the tail trying to pre-shell it.

**Skip the surf-and-turf.** The steak on Anegada is fine. It is also the wrong instinct. The lobster is the dinner. Putting a steak next to it dilutes both. Order a second lobster for the table to share before you order a steak.

**Drink rosé or a crisp white.** The lobster is sweet, the butter is rich, and a Caribbean rum punch - which is otherwise the correct drink for almost any moment in the BVI - will overwhelm it. The Anegada Reef has a Provençal rosé on the list that works. Potter's has a Sauvignon Blanc that does the job. Cow Wreck has whatever cold white they got in on the supply boat that week, and that is fine. Save the rum punch for after.

**Order one extra side of butter.** You will use it. Two if your table is more than four people.

**Pace yourself.** A whole lobster ashore is a 90-minute commitment minimum. The kitchen does not rush, and you should not either. Do not book a return tender pickup at 9:30pm if your reservation is at 7. We've made that mistake. The tender driver waited and was lovely about it; we still felt bad.

## What dinner looks like, end to end

The boat is anchored a half-mile offshore in Setting Point, where the water shifts from deep teal to the turquoise that everyone tries to photograph and nobody quite captures. You take the dinghy in at 6:30, riding low across flat water with the sun behind you, and you walk up the dock past the fishing boats coming in for the night - pangas mostly, paint flaking, names hand-lettered, the day's catch sloshing in plastic totes on the foredeck. The grill is already smoking before you reach the shack. They cook over coconut husks, not charcoal, which gives the meat a faint sweetness and a smoke note you don't get anywhere else; you'll smell it before you see the building.

The lobster comes out whole on a metal tray, split lengthwise down the back, the shell already crackled where the heat got to it. Butter in a small ramekin, lime in wedges, a wedge of grilled johnnycake on the side, a pile of slaw nobody really came for. You eat with your hands. Someone at the table says the thing they always say about the lobster, which is some version of "I had no idea." You walk back to the dinghy in flip-flops, in the dark, with the smell of woodsmoke and lime in your hair, and the tender ride back to the boat under stars is the part everyone tells the story about for years afterward. The BVI lobster dinner is not actually about the lobster. It's about the half-mile of black water between the dock and the boat with the dinner still on your hands.

## A note on the closed season

If you sail with us during the April-through-July window, the Anegada lobster fishery is closed by BVI regulation. This is a real conservation rule with real enforcement - fishermen who pull traps during the closure lose their licenses. The lobster on the menus during those months is held-over frozen tail from the open season. It is still good. It does not compare. We are honest about this when guests ask, which they do.

If your sailing week falls in that window, treat Anegada as a different kind of day. The fresh fish daily catch at the Anegada Reef is excellent - wahoo, mahi, whatever came over the rail that morning. The bonefish flats on the south side of the island are some of the best in the Caribbean, and Anegada is one of the few places in the BVI where a flats guide is genuinely worth the half-day. The long pink beach at Loblolly is still long and still pink. The rum bar at Big Bamboo is open and the rum is cold. Don't go in with the lobster expectation. Go in for the island, and we'll adjust the menu and the day accordingly.
